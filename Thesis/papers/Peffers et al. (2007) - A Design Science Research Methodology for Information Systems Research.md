## In one sentence

Peffers et al. formalise Design Science Research as a six-step nominal process model (DSRM) for IS, providing both a methodological framework for conducting artifact-oriented research and a shared mental model that allows reviewers to recognise and evaluate such work — filling the absence of a commonly accepted DS methodology in the IS discipline.

## Core Ideas
- Design Science Research (DSR) produces explicitly applicable artifacts (constructs, models, methods, instantiations) as research output — distinct from theory-building or theory-testing paradigms borrowed from social/natural sciences
- The DSRM six steps: (1) problem identification and motivation, (2) objectives definition, (3) design and development, (4) demonstration, (5) evaluation, (6) communication
- Multiple research entry points are valid — not all DS research starts at step 1; researchers may enter mid-process (e.g., from an existing solution or observation)
- The methodology is not a rigid orthodoxy — context-specific variants are legitimate as long as they are well-justified

## Methods
- Literature synthesis across engineering, computer science, and IS design research traditions
- Demonstration via four case studies: health assessment database, software reuse measure, Internet video telephony, IS planning method
- Evaluation against three stated objectives: consistency with prior literature, provision of a process model, provision of a mental model for presentation and evaluation

## Key findings — cite these
- DSRM provides a six-step process model applicable to artifact-oriented IS research with multiple valid entry points
- Demonstrated across four distinct IS artifact types — showing generalisability of the methodology across problem domains
- The absence of a shared DS mental model has historically caused DS research to be misidentified as consulting or practice activity — DSRM addresses this directly

## Direct quotes — copy verbatim, include page/section
> "The DS process includes six steps: problem identification and motivation, definition of the objectives for a solution, design and development, demonstration, evaluation, and communication." (Abstract, p. 46)

> "Without a strong component that produces explicitly applicable research solutions, IS research faces the potential of losing influence over research streams for which such applicability is an important value." (p. 47)

> "The DSRM should not be used as a rigid orthodoxy to criticize work that does not follow it explicitly." (Conclusion, p. 74)

## What this paper does NOT cover (gap it leaves)

DSRM is a general-purpose IS methodology not tailored to AI system design — it provides no guidance on how to evaluate artifacts that combine ML forecasting with LLM orchestration, nor on how to benchmark computational constraints (RAM) as a design criterion, which are the specific evaluation challenges of this thesis.

## Strength
- Published in JMIS (AIS Senior Scholar Basket of Eight) — among the highest-prestige IS outlets, ensuring full methodological legitimacy at CBS
- Demonstrated on four diverse case studies, establishing broad applicability across IS artifact types

## Weaknesses
- Six-step process is presented as linear but real DS research is iterative — the paper acknowledges this but does not formalise the iteration
- No guidance specific to AI/ML artifacts — evaluation criteria for algorithmic systems require adaptation beyond what DSRM prescribes

## My critical assessment
- **Strengths:** Establishes a widely accepted and rigorous methodological framework (DSRM) for artifact-oriented IS research, providing clear structure (six-step process) and strong legitimacy for design science approaches — particularly valuable for positioning applied, system-building theses like yours within academic standards.
- **Weaknesses:** The model is presented as nominally sequential and high-level, offering limited operational guidance for iterative development, experimental benchmarking, or evaluation of complex AI systems (e.g., ML + LLM pipelines).
- **Relevance to thesis:** Directly underpins our methodological approach, justifying the design, implementation, and evaluation of a multi-agent predictive system as a valid research contribution; the six-step DSRM maps cleanly onto your pipeline (problem → design → benchmarking → evaluation).
