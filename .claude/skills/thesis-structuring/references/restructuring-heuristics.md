# Restructuring Heuristics

## Threshold: When to Restructure

Restructuring is only justified when one or more of the following is true:

1. **Research question shifted** — the central RQ changed scope, direction, or type (exploratory → explanatory, etc.)
2. **Methodology changed substantially** — paradigm shift, new data source, fundamentally different strategy
3. **Empirical/conceptual balance flipped** — was theory-heavy, now data-driven, or vice versa
4. **New central theoretical lens** — a new framework now dominates the entire argument
5. **Technical section deserves a chapter** — implementation grew complex enough to stand alone
6. **Evaluation/results logic changed** — what was one chapter now has distinct enough phases to split
7. **Systemic structural problems** — redundancy, conceptual overlap across multiple chapters, fragmentation
8. **Supervisor feedback implies misalignment** — explicit structural critique, not just content feedback

Superficial changes (new papers, refined wording, minor scope adjustments) do NOT trigger restructuring.

---

## Operation-Level Rules

### Rename a section when:
- The current name misrepresents the actual content
- The thesis archetype uses a different conventional label
- Supervisor or field convention clearly favors another term
- **Do not rename** just to vary language or sound more sophisticated

### Split a section when:
- Two clearly distinct concepts are collapsed under one heading
- One section has grown so large it creates argumentative imbalance
- The two parts have different methodological or theoretical roles
- **Do not split** micro-topics that belong together conceptually

### Merge sections when:
- Two sections cover the same concept from marginally different angles
- Separation creates redundancy across chapters
- The content of one section is too thin to stand alone
- **Do not merge** sections with genuinely different functions

### Move a section when:
- Its current position breaks the logical flow of the argument
- It belongs earlier (context missing) or later (result not yet established)
- **Do not move** sections just to match another thesis's order

### Add a chapter when:
- A new major contribution area emerged that doesn't fit anywhere
- The thesis archetype requires a phase not currently represented
- A technical or empirical component grew too large for a subsection
- **Do not add chapters** for minor additions or to seem more comprehensive

### Remove a section when:
- It duplicates another section's content
- It is a placeholder with no actual planned content
- The thesis scope narrowed and the section no longer fits
- **Do not remove** sections that are thin but still argumentatively necessary

---

## Continuity Rules

- Preserve chapter numbers where possible to avoid disorientation
- When renaming, note the old name in the rationale
- Avoid changing more than 30% of the outline in a single restructuring
- If major restructuring is needed, propose it explicitly and ask for confirmation before treating it as final

## Common Structural Mistakes to Flag

| Mistake | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| Theory in both Ch2 and Ch5 | Duplicate concept — consolidate or distinguish clearly |
| "Literature Review" + "Theoretical Framework" + "Conceptual Framework" all present | Likely one chapter too many — clarify roles and merge if overlapping |
| "Methodology" has 6+ subsections | May need to split into Research Design and Methods |
| "Discussion" and "Analysis" both present | Check if they're truly separate; often Discussion synthesizes Analysis |
| "Results" in a qualitative thesis | Consider renaming to "Findings" or "Analysis" |
| "Implementation" buried in Methodology | Likely deserves its own chapter if substantial |
| Conclusion chapter missing limitations or future work | Flag as open structural question |

## Standard Chapter Role Definitions

Use these to resolve naming ambiguity:

- **Literature Review**: synthesizes existing research; identifies gap
- **Theoretical Framework**: specifies the theoretical lens applied in this thesis
- **Conceptual Framework**: operationalizes concepts for this study (may include model or diagram)
- **Research Design / Methodology**: overall research strategy, paradigm, approach
- **Methods / Data Collection**: specific techniques used to gather data
- **Analysis / Findings**: what was done with the data, what emerged
- **Results**: outcomes of analysis (more common in quantitative / technical)
- **Discussion**: interpretation, comparison with literature, implications
- **Conclusion**: summary, limitations, future work, contributions

These roles can and should inform whether a section is correctly placed and named.
