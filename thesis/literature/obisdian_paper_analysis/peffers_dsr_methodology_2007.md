## Core argument
Design-science research in IS has lacked a commonly accepted process model, causing inconsistent execution and reporting. The DSRM process model provides a six-step methodology (problem identification → objectives → design → demonstration → evaluation → communication) that is applicable across multiple entry points depending on the research context.

## Method
Literature synthesis of existing DS and IS research processes, combined with case analysis of three exemplar IS design-science projects. Proposes a nominal process model with six activities and four possible entry points, validated through application to the three cases.

## Key finding
The DSRM process model provides a clear template for planning and communicating design-science research, enabling both researchers and reviewers to assess methodological rigour. Entry-point flexibility makes it applicable to problem-motivated, objective-motivated, design-motivated, and context-motivated research.

## Relevance to thesis
- Ch.3 (Methodology): the DSRM six-step process directly structures the thesis's research process — each of the seven thesis phases maps to one or more DSRM activities
- Provides the standard citation pair alongside Hevner et al. (2004) for establishing methodological legitimacy of the design-science approach
- Evaluation activity in DSRM maps to the thesis's three-level validation framework (SRQ3)

## Gap / limitation
DSRM was developed before the era of AI/ML artefacts and does not address iterative evaluation loops typical of ML benchmarking or the challenge of evaluating emergent agent behaviour.
