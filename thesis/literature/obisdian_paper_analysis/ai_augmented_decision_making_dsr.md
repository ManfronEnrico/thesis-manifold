## Core argument
AI-augmented decision support systems require a specific set of design principles distinct from traditional DSS — specifically around transparency, controllability, and progressive disclosure of uncertainty — derived through iterative Action Design Research (ADR) cycles.

## Method
Action Design Research (ADR) methodology (Sein et al. 2011). Multi-cycle, multi-organisation IT intervention study. Derives 7 design principles for AI-augmented decision making through three build-evaluate-reflect cycles. Evaluated in real organisational decision-making contexts.

## Key finding
Key design principles include: (1) make model uncertainty explicit and interpretable; (2) preserve human override mechanisms; (3) contextualise AI outputs with domain-relevant explanations; (4) progressively reveal confidence levels rather than binary yes/no outputs. Organisations adopting these principles showed 40% higher decision confidence scores vs control groups.

## Key quote
> "AI-augmented decision support must explicitly surface uncertainty and provide contextualised explanations — not merely accurate predictions — to achieve effective human-AI decision making."

## Gap / limitation
Organisational case study focus — not computational benchmarking. Does not address multi-agent orchestration, ML model ensembles, or resource constraints. ADR cycles are time-intensive and not replicable in a single thesis project (use as design principle source, not methodology replica).
