## Core argument
Design science produces IT artefacts (constructs, models, methods, instantiations) intended to solve real-world organisational problems, and IS research must address both the utility of artefacts and the rigour of their evaluation. The paper presents seven guidelines for conducting and evaluating design-science research in IS.

## Method
Conceptual framework development based on prior IS and engineering literature. Proposes a dual-cycle model contrasting behavioural-science (truth) and design-science (utility) paradigms, and provides seven concrete guidelines covering artefact design, problem relevance, design evaluation, research contribution, rigour, design as search process, and research communication.

## Key finding
Design-science research is legitimate and necessary in IS, but must be evaluated against clearly stated utility criteria — not just internal validity. The seven guidelines provide a structured methodology for planning, executing, and reporting IS design-science studies.

## Relevance to thesis
- Foundational justification for the thesis's design-science epistemological stance — the multi-agent framework is an IS artefact evaluated against utility criteria (accuracy, decision quality, resource efficiency)
- Ch.3 (Methodology): provides the methodological scaffolding and the language of "artefact types" used throughout the thesis
- Directly informs how the thesis frames its research design, evaluation strategy, and contribution claims

## Gap / limitation
Guidelines are general-purpose and do not address the specific challenges of AI/ML artefacts, where evaluation requires statistical benchmarks and behavioural testing beyond traditional IS utility assessment.
